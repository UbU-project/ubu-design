# Niko’s Tuesday

**Status:** Derived far-horizon narrative — a projection of the full-product (Phase 2/3+) user experience; canonical design files win on any conflict

The room was still dark when Niko surfaced, and his first thought was that he hadn't slept right. Third night in a row. He lay there a while doing the arithmetic he always did — how many hours, how badly he'd pay for it by two in the afternoon, whether coffee could carry the difference.

When he finally reached for the phone on the crate beside the mattress and opened UbU, it didn't show him the usual wall of green. The shift was still there at nine; that wasn't going anywhere. But the morning around it had been loosened, like a belt let out a notch. The run he'd planned was gone, folded down into a walk. The dense two-hour study block — conduit-bending diagrams for his apprenticeship exam — had been trimmed to forty minutes and pushed later, after food and coffee, to the part of the morning when he might actually keep any of it in his head.

He hadn't told it he slept badly. UbU knew because of the things he'd let it watch, and only those things: the restless heart rate, the screen still on at one in the morning, the water glass he never refilled. It had put those together, reached the obvious conclusion, and quietly rebuilt the day to fit the body that was going to have to live it. His old calendar used to know where he was supposed to be. This thing seemed to know where he actually stood.

And everything it did, it did right there on the phone in his hand. Nothing about his bad night left the room. That had mattered to him from the start — more than the features, more than anything — that the machine doing the thinking sat in his pocket and answered to him and to no one upstairs.

---

Inside UbU there was one honest picture of his life, and everything else asked that picture for permission before it acted. His money, his hours, his energy, the tools in the cupboard, the half-learned skills, the things he was trying to become — all of it in one place, kept straight, never sold sideways. When he'd first set it up he'd half expected the usual: an account on some company's servers, a firm that now knew him better than his own mother and rented the knowing out to strangers. There was no company on the other end. There was no other end. There was just the picture, and it was his, and nobody was making money off the back of it.

He got up, walked, drank the water it had nagged about without nagging. At the counter it suggested eggs — protein, slow burn, you'll thank yourself at noon. He looked at the eggs, looked at the price of eggs, and had toast instead. UbU didn't argue. It logged the toast, adjusted the rest of the day a hair to account for the dip it now expected around eleven, and moved on. That was the part he'd never gotten used to with anything else he'd ever owned: when he told it no, it simply heard the no. It planned *around* him. It never planned *over* him.

The forty minutes of study went better than the two hours would have. He could feel the difference between knowing where his hands went on a length of pipe and knowing it in his fingers. UbU could only tell him *what* to practice and *when*; the technique itself had to be wrung out of his own arms, one bend at a time. It had no illusions about that, and neither did he.

---

The trouble started, as it usually did, with the warehouse.

His shift was a nine, fine. But the company's scheduling software had decided, in its own logic, to put him on the late shift tonight and the early one tomorrow — barely six hours between clocking out and clocking back in, half of that the sleep he wouldn't get. He'd worked that turnaround before. He knew exactly the man it made of him by Thursday: short, clumsy, dangerous around the forklifts.

He didn't have to argue it. He'd never told the company about his sleep, or his heart rate, or the way two short nights stacked into something dangerous around heavy machinery. He never would. But months ago he'd set a line for himself inside UbU — a small, hard rule that said *not those two shifts back to back, not ever* — and he'd handed that one rule, and only that rule, out to stand at the boundary between his world and theirs.

So when the company's system reached across to book him, UbU checked the request against that line and turned it down. Not a tantrum. Not a leaked diary of why. The company's system got back exactly one thing: *not allowed.* It couldn't see his sleep. It couldn't see his health, his moods, the shape of his week. It had no idea there even *was* a reason, only that the door was shut and the rule behind it wasn't theirs to open. The protection crossed the wall between him and his employer; everything that made it personal stayed home.

There was a clean version of this he liked. The rule could refuse, but it could never be quietly switched off from the far side. If it ever came to it, he could pull the whole arrangement out by the root — walk, take his footing with him — but no one out there could reach in and undo it. The line held in the only direction that mattered.

The early shift stayed. The turnaround dissolved. Somewhere a different worker got asked instead, and he felt a small twist about that, but the rule was the rule and he'd worn the consequences of not having one.

---

He still wanted out of the long evening shift, though — he'd rather trade it for a daytime one he could study around. So he handed the errand to UbU.

"Find me someone to trade with this week," he told it, and drew the box small with his own hands. Only these three coworkers, the ones he trusted. Don't commit me to anything past Thursday. Don't trade away a morning. Come back before you say yes to anything real. It went off to arrange it on his behalf, wearing his name but only the slice of it he'd lent, and every message it sent in his voice he could read after, line by line. If he didn't like where it was going he could call it back mid-sentence. The errand was the machine's. The authority was always, only, his — handed out in thin scoped slivers, never surrendered.

While it worked, a contractor he'd applied to wanted proof he could actually do the trade. The old way, that meant handing over everything — references, full history, your whole self laid out for a stranger to paw through. Instead he sent a single thing: a verified record of the hours he'd logged and the certifications he'd passed, sealed so it couldn't be faked, carrying nothing else about him. The contractor could check it was real. The contractor learned not one fact more.

Those records were his to carry anywhere, and that quiet fact had changed how it felt to be at the warehouse at all. He wasn't trapped there. His proof of who he was and what he could do didn't live on the company's servers, to be lost or held hostage the day he tried to leave. It lived with him. Which meant when something was unfair he could say so, because leaving was a door and not a wall. He'd never had to use that, exactly. But the boss seemed to know it was true, and treated him a degree better than he treated the ones who couldn't.

The arrangement came back: his long Tuesday evening for a coworker's Wednesday daytime, clean, nobody worse off. When he said yes, his whole week had to be re-thought around it — study blocks, the exam countdown, the walk he'd talked himself into. The phone got warm in his hand for a few seconds while UbU chewed through every version of the week and laid the best one down in front of him. All of that thinking happened in his palm. He found that almost funny — the heaviest computer he owned was the one nobody else could log into.

---

The afternoon shift was the afternoon shift. Pick, scan, lift, repeat. Beside him, a coworker kept swearing at his phone. The guy ran his life out of the ordinary apps — a calendar here, a reminders list there, the company's own software, each a separate window into a separate firm's plans for him, none of them talking to the others. And every few minutes one of them lit up to sell him something: a watch, a course, a better phone, every ad aimed with uncanny precision at the things he'd glanced at once and regretted. "It knows I'm broke and it won't shut up about it," the guy said, thumbing an ad shut for the third time and losing his place in the pick list. The interruptions cost him seconds, then minutes, then his temper. By four he was a man being managed by his own pocket.

Niko's pocket was quiet. UbU didn't interrupt him because UbU had nothing to sell him and no one to sell him *to*. Nobody downstream was watching the shape of his afternoon, packaging it up, shipping it off to strangers who'd pay for a clean shot at his attention. The endless low hum of being measured for someone else's profit — the thing the other man's phone did all day without either of them quite naming it — simply wasn't happening in his pocket. He'd stopped noticing the absence the way you stop noticing a toothache once it's finally gone, until a day like this set the noise down right beside him and he remembered what it had cost him to live inside it.

He didn't say any of that out loud. It wasn't a contest, and the other man wasn't a fool — he just hadn't found the way out yet, or hadn't believed there was one. Most people hadn't. The free apps were free because you were the thing being sold, and that bargain was so old most people had stopped hearing it as a bargain at all.

On the walk home the bike question caught up with him. The bottom bracket had been grinding for a week and now it was a noise you couldn't unhear. He'd been dreading the decision because deciding meant money, and money meant the thing where you talk yourself into the worst option because it's the one you can stand to look at.

So he had UbU lay the numbers out honest. It knew what the bike was worth now and what it'd be worth in a year, knew what he actually had in the account this minute, knew the real lifetime cost of the shiny new electric one whose ad had been chasing his coworker around all afternoon and would have chased Niko too, once. Laid out plainly, the financing deal on the new one cost nearly double the sticker by the end, in small bites designed not to sting. The repair cost a part and an afternoon. There was no spin on it, no nudge toward the option that paid someone a commission — because no one had paid UbU to nudge. Just the true shape of the money, the way a friend who was good with money would tell it to you. He'd repair.

And he could repair it himself, which a year ago wouldn't have been true. He had the technique now — learned it through the same app that was learning him — and the part was cheap, and the one tool he was missing, a bearing press, he didn't have to buy.

---

The kid two floors down, Toma, had a bearing press. Toma had it because Niko's own impact driver lived in Toma's flat half the time. They lent back and forth without keeping score, the way neighbors who like each other do, and the only thing UbU did there was make sure the lending didn't quietly hurt either of them — that Niko didn't hand over a tool he'd need for his own exam build that weekend, that Toma got it back before he needed it. It looked after both ends. It would no more let Niko strand himself than let him strand Toma. Each of them could see the other was good for it — a small, plain record of borrowings returned — and could see nothing else. Enough to trust. Not enough to pry.

That was the free layer, the neighborly one. There was the other layer too, the one with money in it, and Niko used that on Sunday evenings: an hour of guitar to a teenager across the block whose mother paid him a fair, small rate. He wasn't a great guitarist. He was a patient one, three chords ahead of his student and honest about the rest, and that turned out to be worth something to somebody. The few coins it brought in weren't going to change his life. But they were dignified coins. He'd earned them by knowing a thing and handing it to someone who didn't, which was the whole shape of every good day he could remember.

He swapped the impact driver for the bearing press at Toma's door, fixed the bottom bracket on a towel on the kitchen floor, got grease under his nails, got the crank to spin silent and true. It took the afternoon the numbers had promised it would. He felt, turning the pedal with one finger and listening to nothing, briefly and completely competent, which is not a feeling the world hands out for free.

---

By evening the day had quietly become a good one, and the strange thing was how little of that was the machine and how much of it was just the day being lived in the right order.

He'd traded out of the bad shift. He'd dodged the predatory loan. He'd fixed the thing with his own hands. And now, at seven, with the exam diagrams left where they belonged for tomorrow's clearer head, there was an unscheduled hour that nobody and nothing wanted anything from. UbU had no plan for it, and nothing else had a claim on it either — no app waiting to fill it, no ad waiting to spend it. That was the point of the whole thing, in the end. Not that it filled his time, but that it gave some back, clean, with no hooks in it.

He spent the hour upstairs at Toma's family's table because they'd asked and he'd said yes. He played the three chords he knew while Toma's little brother sang the words wrong on purpose. Nobody's device was on the table, lit up, listening. His sister called halfway through and he stepped onto the landing to talk her through a form she was stuck on, and the phone in his pocket stayed a phone in his pocket, because some help you give with your own attention or not at all.

Lying down that night — earlier than last night, UbU had seen to that without making a thing of it — he thought about the line he'd held that morning, the one nobody could reach in and switch off. His old calendar had always known where he was supposed to be. He'd spent years being exactly where he was supposed to be and feeling, underneath it, lost, and watched, and gently sold to. The difference now was small and he could barely have named it. It was something like footing. The day had asked him a dozen times what he wanted, and waited for the answer, and built itself around it, and never once turned the answer into a product. Toast over eggs. This shift not that one. Repair not replace. His sister over the screen.

He still woke up tired. He still worked a warehouse. He still didn't have much. But he knew, lying there, exactly where he stood — and that, it turned out, had been most of what he was missing.

He slept.
